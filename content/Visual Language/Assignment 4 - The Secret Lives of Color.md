

To me the most interesting section was the describing of a color as a intensely labor and resource heavy process. When we think of the color today and the consequences of choosing one color over another, a lot of consequences of choosing a certain color is tied with historical, political, social or religious meanings and consequences. A paper that supports the republican paper would probably stray away from using blue in a piece about victory for the party, for example. But is never the environmental impact or consequences of a color discussed. When I see the beautiful colors of my favorite stores, did I ever consider what the color cost? The podcast discussed pigments made by crushing thousands of animals, almost to the brink of extinction. As people look for more and more ways to differentiate or find the brightest pink paint, what new ecosystem will be ruined in the process?

A second interesting point was how much of our understanding and perception of color is socially embedded in us instead of cognitively. Red is anger and love, pink is feminine, and, according to the podcast, so was blue at a time due to the Virgin Mary. When they are switched we feel a sense of confusion. It makes me wonder how many colors we love or hate that were more imprinted on us as opposed to our genuine enjoyment of them.

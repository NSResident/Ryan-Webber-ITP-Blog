### Submission

Watching this episode of Abstract brought to mind two intriguing points. The first is how an understanding of Typography adds an overlay on the world that is felt but hardly seen in daily life. The episode does a great job in highlighting the different font families, their weight, and even size on storefronts, newspapers, screens, etc. Many of the places in the show I have walked by without looking twice at the font, even if I was drawn to the storefront or chose that book out of the hundreds of books at a store. Though we don't realize the minute differences in fonts such as the kerning or the size of ascenders, I was surprised to notice ligatures for the first time. 

Second, was how much intention, meaning, and design goes into a typeface. The episode showcased this from two angles: Reversing the concept of a typeface and creating a new typeface that fits a theme. A large amount of the episode was dedicated to the observation and analysis of typefaces used in older watches where Jonathan Hoefler tries to piece together the original concept of a font by studying the curvatures and spacings of different numbers and letter. He likens this to an archaeologist reconstructing the anatomy of a dinosaur from finding its bones, seeing the part and understanding the whole. The incredible part is how a larger concept does in fact manifest itself into letter spacing, angles, and even the relational size between lowercase and uppercase. This is further emphasized when Hoefler explains creating the Parliament, Guggenheim, or Knockout fonts, that the original concept and purpose of the font great impacted its final design, whether multiple versions were needed, and whether it can be used in both physical and digital visuals.

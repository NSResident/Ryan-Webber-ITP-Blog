

In this introductory week, we covered the following topics:
* Course overview 
* Prompt for the first assignment
* Basics in operating a Zoom recorder
* The power of sound and music to set and change the feeling from a visual

This last bullet was particularly interesting to me since it was precisely the interplay of sound and visuals that brought me to ITP in the first place. Diving deeper into sound, we had two readings, that gave me a new perspective on the importance of sound and the act of listening.

In Pauline Oliveros' TED Talk about Deep Listening, this particular quote stuck out to me:

***Humans have developed consensual agreements on the interpretations of sound waves delivered to the brain by the ear. Languages are such agreements... Scientists can measure what happens in the ear, measuring listening is another matter as it involves subjectivity.***

At it's core, sound is really just waves. How we move them into categories like noise, music, conversation, etc. is a grouping that was done ages before me and will likely last long after me. But even this is divided. I live on a busy street where you hear the buses and garbage trucks pass by every hour, birds chirping, excited conversations, music, and even people BBQing on their stoop. In the daytime it energizes me and reminds me of the life of the city, but in the night, right before I go to bed, it becomes noise. Someone who has never lived in a city might just consider it all just noise. All of it is, at the end of the day subjective and relational.

But it's also that loosely defined nature of sound that gives it all its meaning. In class, Prof. Santana showed us two videos: The opening theme of Different Strokes and a remix done by an editor. The former was a happy, cheerful, and celebratory tune that helped frame the show as a positive and heartwarming sitcom. The remix, however, was dark and eerie. Reminiscent of Jaws, the violins made the class tense, waiting for disaster at the next cut, even though the video sequence for both were the same. This ability to completely alter the mood and environment, even when all else remains the same, is the power of sound.

In an interview with Haley Shaw, she describes sound design as *t**he way to shape an intended feeling through sound and music***.  To achieve this, Haley of course applies a variety of audio creation and editing techniques, but perhaps more importantly she approaches every sound with intention. What is the audience supposed to feel? When does the video transition? Is the music supposed to highlight or suppress a visual element? Are we leaning into or avoiding a cliché? These questions and more determine what, when, how and where a sound or song is played. Similar to Oliveros' Deep Listening, the difference is in the intention. As designers (or future designers?) we will undoubtedly practice and perfect techniques, constantly build our knowledge, and become proficient in any number of tools, but without intention the beautiful songs around us become ambient background noise, and our more beautiful creations will be out of place and in disagreement with the bigger picture we operate in.

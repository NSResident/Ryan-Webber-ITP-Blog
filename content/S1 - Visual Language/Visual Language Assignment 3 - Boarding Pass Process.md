
### Process

The first challenge was trying to decide which software to use. InDesign seemed like a good option since this was going to be printed and had to be aligned properly, but honestly I couldn't figure out how most of the options worked. I decided to take a break and attend the Design Lab's Illustrator workshop. After about 2 hours of learning Illustrator and practicing a few helpful commands, I felt comfortable enough to attack this again.

My first attempt was just to make the shape of the boarding pass and layout some text to get a sense of the size when printed:

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/IMG_4867.jpeg)

it was WAY too large, and I realized the dashes look oddly shaped so I adjusted both:

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/IMG_4868.jpeg)

Now with a better size, I added more of the text and started playing with different layout options. I also added the barcodes and Delta iconography, as well as update some of the spacing to fit the new content:

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/IMG_4869.jpeg)

At this point, it was starting to feel much more official. I decided to lay out all the information I generally need to see quickly at the top of the pass, while making sure the boarding time and depart time were clearly indicated. Still, the pass felt a little soulless. The next step was adding more content in the tear-away section, adding some imagery, and updating the font to be sharper yet easy to read:

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/IMG_4870.jpeg)

One step at a time, my boarding pass was starting to take shape. This iteration took the longest, as I was trying to figure out how to properly layer and add transparency to the Delta logo. Experimenting with fonts also took quite a long time, and I settled on a single font with different sizes and weight to call out more important information. It was getting late, and I was running out of ways to make the pass more attractive, so I quickly jotted down some ideas and decided to work on it in the morning. 

The break was definitely a great idea, as I woke up with more ideas on how to add color and life to the boarding pass. Luckily, my roommate keeps all his boarding passes, so I was able to get some design and layout inspiration with physical passes. After a lot of tutorials and google searches, I came up with 3 good options (some minor errors remained but were fixed in the final version):

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/Pasted%20image%2020250920123108.png)

The biggest additions were the faded sky as a background for the main pass, the Delta-blue fill on the tear-away, updates to the typography, and adding a different Delta logo in the tear-away to keep the distinctive red. Finally, I settled with option #2. I felt that the combination of the logo on the right and the horizontal layout of the ticket information really balanced out the remaining negative space.

![](https://PCompPull.b-cdn.net/Visual%20Language%20Boarding%20Pass/pass.png)

There's still a lot of room for improvement here. On the overall shape, I think the "perforated" part could be tweaked to look more realistic and the inside content of the pass could be scale down to give more breathing room on the left margin. On typography, I think the dark blue and black text combination is slightly jarring sometimes, just using one color or a sparingly-applied second color may make more sense. 

Through this assignment, I became really familiar with Illustrator and it's surface level capabilities. It was tough at first to understand how to manipulate shapes and images as vectors, but I'm happy with the result. Lastly, I want to thank the Design Lab and Proud for the workshop, without it, I would have been completely lost.
